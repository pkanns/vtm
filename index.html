Vidai to Mulai, From Start to Finish
Why this exists

Most tools count tasks.
We are trying to notice what happened.

There is a gap between handing someone a task and knowing how it actually went. Most platforms close a ticket and move on. That gap is where the real story lives, and most tools never look at it.

Mulai starts with the gig. A single piece of work, with a name, a person who placed it, a person who is doing it, a beginning and an end. Nothing more is assumed. Every gig carries its own small reflection at the end, a few honest questions asked of both people involved.

We are not measuring busyness. The clock has done that for too long, and not particularly well. We are trying to build a quieter record, one that remembers how work actually felt and went, for the people who did it.

How a gig moves

Three steps, nothing hidden.

From the moment work is written down to the moment it is understood.

01
Place

A gig is written down. A name, who it is for, who will do it, when it should start and finish. If it repeats, it is set up once and looks after itself from there. If it cannot wait, it starts straight away.

02
Do

The work happens. Time is logged as it goes, or afterwards if that is easier. The gig moves from waiting to in progress. Nothing about it disappears once it is done.

03
Reflect

Both people sit with the finished gig and answer a few honest questions, about how it went and how it felt to work together. The final word belongs to the person who placed the work. This is the part most tools skip. For us, it is where the value sits.

What's Built

Quiet things, working.

Everything below is live, used today, on real work.

Structure
A place for everything, named simply

Every gig sits inside a project, and carries its own short code so you always know what it is and where it belongs, without opening it. Nothing is ever placed without context.

Accountability
One person placed it, one person is doing it

Every gig has both. Each person sees only what is theirs to see, no more, no less. Nobody is guessing who is responsible for what.

Reflection
A few honest questions, every time

How the work felt and how it went, asked of both people. The final word rests with whoever placed the gig. What this is worth, in money or in anything else, is a separate question for another day. The reflection stands on its own.

Time
The clock, kept honestly

Start with one tap, your place and time noted without being asked. Stop the same way. If you forgot, add it afterwards. Either way, your time is yours to look back on, simply.

Recurrence
Work that repeats, without being repeated

Set a gig up once if it happens every week or every month, and it takes care of itself from there. The person doing it can change for next time without disturbing this time.

Care
Looked after, quietly

Signed in, and signed out if you step away for too long, with a gentle warning first. Nothing dramatic. Just looked after.

An open door
